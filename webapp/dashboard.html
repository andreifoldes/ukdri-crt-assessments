The mathematics exactly what the dashboard computes

ICC(2,1) — the forest-plot statistic

Each participant is measured on k = 2 attempts. A two-way ANOVA splits the total variation into three mean squares — between participants (rows), between attempts (columns), and residual noise — and the two-way random, single-measurement, absolute-agreement ICC combines them as:

ICC(2,1) = MSR − MSE MSR + (k−1)·MSE + kn(MSC − MSE)

  • MSR — between-participants mean square (the true signal we want)
  • MSC — between-attempts mean square (systematic drift from attempt 1 to 2)
  • MSE — residual mean square (measurement noise)
  • n — number of participants with both attempts  ·  k = 2

It reads as the share of total variance that reflects real differences between people rather than measurement error. The (k⁄n)(MSC − MSE) term keeps any systematic attempt-to-attempt shift in the denominator — so two attempts that are perfectly correlated but offset (everyone scores 2 points higher the second time) score lower than a plain correlation would. That is the "absolute agreement" property: the dashboard rewards measurements that land on the same number, not just ones that rise and fall together.

The two-way ANOVA on these 8 numbers (grand mean = 12.125) gives the three mean squares — MSR = 35.46 (participants are well spread out), MSC = 0.125 (attempt 1 and 2 averages barely differ), MSE = 2.125 (small residual noise). With n = 4, k = 2:

ICC = 35.46 − 2.125 35.46 + 2.125 + (2⁄4)(0.125 − 2.125) = 33.3336.58 = 0.91

ICC ≈ 0.91 → excellent agreement: the gap between a person's two attempts is tiny compared with how much people differ from each other.

The whiskers are a 95% confidence interval — an F-based interval (McGraw & Wong 1996, case A,1) with Satterthwaite degrees of freedom. The F-quantiles are evaluated from the regularised incomplete beta function in js/stats.js, so the page needs no statistics library. This is the same number R's irr::icc("twoway","agreement","single") returns.

Bland–Altman — the agreement plot

For each paired measurement (xi, yi) — two attempts, or two instruments — we take the difference and the average:

di = xi − yi · mi = xi + yi2

then plot di against mi and draw three reference lines:

  • bias = d̄ — the mean difference (systematic over- or under-estimation)
  • limits of agreement = d̄ ± 1.96·SD(d) — the band that holds ≈95% of differences

Worked example — the same 4 participants

The differences d = x − y are −2, +2, −2, +1. From those four numbers alone:

  • bias = mean(d) = (−2 + 2 − 2 + 1) ⁄ 4 = −0.25
  • SD(d) = sample standard deviation of the differences = 2.06
  • limits of agreement = −0.25 ± 1.96 × 2.06 = [−4.3, +3.8]

A bias of just −0.25 means there is no real systematic drift between attempts; the band [−4.3, +3.8] is where ≈95% of individual attempt-to-attempt differences land.

A correlation tells you whether two measures move together; Bland–Altman tells you how far apart they actually are, and whether that gap grows with the size of the score. For cross-instrument pairs on different scales (e.g. PSQI 0–21 vs ESS 0–24) each measure is converted to a z-score first, so the difference is unit-free and the bias is interpretable.
